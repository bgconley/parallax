# Design Authority Hierarchy

## Level 1 — Canonical anchors
These are the approved visual truth and must be treated as the strongest examples.

- `anchors/01_today.png`
- `anchors/02_quick_capture.png`
- `anchors/03_reset_from_here.png`
- `anchors/04_break_it_down.png`
- `anchors/05_timing_session.png`
- `anchors/06_timing_review.png`
- `anchors/07_calibrate_timing_sheet.png`

What they define:
- spacing rhythm
- title/subtitle treatment
- card softness and density
- CTA hierarchy
- emotional tone
- list row grammar
- helper AI card tone
- temporal learning presentation

## Level 2 — Source docs
These define the intended system semantics and should win over image ambiguity.

Most important:
- `source_docs/02_canonical_design_language.md`
- `source_docs/03_information_architecture_and_navigation.md`
- `source_docs/04_surface_design_specs.md`
- `source_docs/05_component_system.md`
- `source_docs/06_interaction_patterns.md`
- `source_docs/07_microcopy_voice_tone.md`
- `source_docs/08_accessibility_cognitive_load.md`
- `source_docs/10_design_anti_patterns.md`
- `source_docs/11_design_acceptance_gates.md`

## Level 3 — Tokens and inventory
Use these to prevent drift in color, spacing, naming, and component family proliferation.

## Level 4 — System boards
Use them as expansion rails, not as higher authority than the anchors or source docs.

## Level 5 — Continuation screens
These are excellent sibling examples, but they are still generated continuation assets. Use them to extend, not to overwrite the existing canon.
